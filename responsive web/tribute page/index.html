ARP 2600 Synthesizer

A game-changer in electronic music history

Introduction

The ARP 2600 is a semi-modular analog subtractive audio synthesizer, produced by ARP Instruments, Inc. between 1971 and 1980. Designed by Alan R. Pearlman, it was one of the first synthesizers to gain widespread use and recognition in the music industry. Its distinctive sound and groundbreaking features made it a popular choice for many artists and musicians throughout the 1970s and beyond.

Design and Features

The ARP 2600 is a patchable, semi-modular synthesizer, meaning that it can be used without patch cables to create a wide range of sounds. However, the addition of patch cables allows for even greater flexibility in signal routing and sound design. Some of its notable features include:

  • Three voltage-controlled oscillators (VCOs)
  • A 24 dB/octave voltage-controlled low-pass filter (VCF)
  • A voltage-controlled amplifier (VCA)
  • Ring modulation
  • Sample and hold
  • An envelope follower
  • A pre-amplifier for external audio input

Influence and Legacy

The ARP 2600 has been used by many prominent artists and musicians, including Stevie Wonder, Pete Townshend of The Who, Jean-Michel Jarre, and Edgar Winter. It was also featured prominently in the 1977 science fiction film, "Close Encounters of the Third Kind", where it was used to communicate with extraterrestrial beings through music.

Over the years, the ARP 2600 has become a highly sought-after instrument for its unique sound and versatility. It has been used in various genres of music, from progressive rock to electronic dance music. Many software synthesizers and hardware recreations have been developed to emulate the sound and functionality of the ARP 2600, including the recent Korg ARP 2600 FS, a full-size reissue of the original.
